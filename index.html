About Me

Hello, I'm Manoel McCadden.

I'm an audio and systems programmer, audio engineer, and audio designer passionate about bridging the gap between audio and technology. I began my programming journey coding in C# and Java for class in high school and as my understanding grew my love deepened. With years of experience in music through concert and jazz band, I began exploring music through a technical lens and found my calling. My goals are developing and improving products/projects through efficiency and cohesion, while adding to the user's experience and immersion.

I am currently entering my junior year at DigiPen Institute of Technology, where I am studying for my Bachelor of Science in Computer Science and Digital Audio. Through my time at DigiPen I have worked on and completed three different team-based game projects and have explored various passions and ideas through independent projects. Through these experiences, I have learned and honed valuable skills in programming (systems, graphics, and audio), teamwork, task management, and project cycles.

Beyond my programming work, I love performing and practicing jazz where I primarily play the Baritone Saxophone. I also adore working in my school's studio, providing audio support for my school's live events, and playing video games with my friends.

Check out my computer science and music projects below, and contact me by email, phone, or through LinkedIn!

WebSynth

A browser-based polyphonic synthesizer using p5.js that features four distinct waveforms and real-time visualization. While initially developed as a tool to help teach high school students the basics of programming, it evolved into a personal project exploring audio synthesis through visualization.

Key Features:
   - 4 selectable waveforms
   - Polyphonic playback for chords and harmonies
   - Interactive drag and drop GUI
   - MIDI-style input using a standard computer keyboard
   - Live visualization of frequency, amplitude, and MIDI note data

A key technical challenge I faced during this project was attempting to mitigate the audible 'clicks' from oscillators as they started and stopped While I implemented solutions such as amplitude ramping and ADSR envelopes, I found that the key issue lay within the lack of granular control given through the p5 framework. Through this experience I have learned valuable information in synthesis visualization and look forward to re-implementing this through C++ and portaudio and/or juce in the future to gain greater control over the audio stream and produce seamless output.
